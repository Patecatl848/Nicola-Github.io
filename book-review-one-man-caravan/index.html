Book Review: One Man Caravan

May 2, 2021 #books #reviews

Robert Edison Fulton was the first solo round-the-world motorcycle tourer. That might sound or not sound impressive, but let me add a little more detail so you can get the whole picture. He made his worldwide trip on a two-cylinder Douglas motorcycle between July 1932 and December 1933, more or less 90 years ago. On his way from London to the colonial Middle East, he crossed Nazi Germany. Some of the countries and places he passed do not exist anymore. Most have changed dramatically; others, not so much. I suspect his adventures in Syria, Afghanistan, or at the Indian-Pakistani borders might have been written today.

When Fulton started his journey, he was in his early twenties. The book was published only a few years later, in 1937. Stories were fresh and unconditioned by the passing age. That is important because, within the next decade, the world would change forever. In Japan, he is greeted by a flock of japan motorcyclists who will accompany him across the country, celebrating the bonds between the United States and the Rising Sun. A bunch of years later, a situation like that would have been next to impossible.

This book is not your typical motorcycle log. It is more about stories, people, and societies. The Author might happily skip thousands of miles across some incredible terrain and places just because he feels the urge to tell us about that memorable encounter he had in Teheran or Tokyo or that otherwise small insignificant Malaysian village.

The tone is often naive and amusing. Even dramatic events are often narrated in a light, entertaining fashion. But make no mistake, this was a genuine, dangerous adventure. It is somewhat startling that the Author could cross so many God-forsaken places only to get his bike stolen when he was back home in New York. Thankfully, he got it back one week later.

In few occurrences, a seemingly colonial attitude surfaces. That might sound jarring to the modern educated reader, but we have to put it all in context. After all, in 1932, Fulton was the offspring of a wealthy American family. He was studying in London when he replied to a dare and then left to explore the world. Given the premise, I think he emerges as a curious, unassuming person who is willing to learn, understand and, of course, explore.

Another young student, Patrick Leight Fermor, travelled across Europe in those years, but on foot. Leigh memories are also available in a three-book series for your reading pleasure1. Together, the stories of these two fearless authors draw a great picture of that age, right before WWII and the following industrial and technological sprawl, when the world was still enormous and split into many, often isolated cultures and societies.
